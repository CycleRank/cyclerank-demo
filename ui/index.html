In Wikipedia, the links between articles constitute a vast concept network, emerging from the collaborative process that involves thousands of users. Previous research has shown how this network can be leveraged to analyze the dominating definition of a topic, shedding light on its boundary, context, and internal structure. Furthermore, the density of connections in the link network makes it possible to reach virtually any other topic in the encyclopedia, starting from a single page. However, not all pages are created equal: clicking on the first link in the main text of an English Wikipedia article and then repeating the subsequent articles leads to the “Philosophy” article in most cases. This over-representation highlights the need for dedicated algorithms to identify the topics most relevant to a given concept. In this demonstration, we compare our approach, Cyclerank, a novel algorithm based on cyclic paths to find the most relevant nodes related to a topic, with well-known algorithms such as PageRank (PR) and Personalized PageRank (PPR). We use this demonstration to show how established algorithms are limited by the fact that they always give a high score to nodes with high in-degree, regardless of the starting node. In contrast, Cyclerank provides more relevant results and solves the problem of setting arbitrary relevance thresholds.
